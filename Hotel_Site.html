Responsive Site for Hotel Booking
Self-Led Independent Project
Project Timespan: 3/23 - 4/23
Responsibilities: UX Research, Sketches, Wireframes, Prototypes
Technology: Adobe XD
Project Overview
This was my personal project, which I started with the goal of making a hotel booking site that was not only easy to use, but thoughtfully designed for all types of users.

After my research, I was able to come up with a flow that aided casual travelers, more frequent travelers, and business travelers alike without being too cluttered or complicated.
Challenges
When starting this project, I was aware that a difficulty with making something like a hotel booking site that is used by such a wide variety of users is managing the needs of all users that may have conflicting user goals.

Another challenge I encountered in making this site was figuring out how to include the many details that are needed for users to have a successful hotel stay with overwhelming the user with cluttered information.
User Research
Since I was making this site as a self-led, independent project, my user research was limited to interviewing different people who I knew used hotel booking sites in the past, and conducting competitive audits.
User Interviews
After interviewing people I knew of various backgrounds, I came up with a few key groups whose needs I needed to keep in mind while designing the site.
Novice Travelers

Know nothing about booking their own trips or hotels, and need a lot of guidance to have a successful hotel stay.
Casual Vacationers

Have some experience booking a hotel and don't need as much help through the process, but may want more knowledge about features and details of their stay.
Business Travelers

Know exactly what they are doing and don't need much aid, just want straightforward information about their room and a streamlined process.
Competitive Audit
After establishing the various users' needs, I then evaluated sites with comparable purposes to see what I could learn from them and improve upon.
Trends I Noticed
- Hotel chain sites all seem to have similar, one-path layouts.
- Layouts tended to have a lot of information on each page.
- It was common for pages to look cluttered.
Research Conclusions
I organized my research into some common or predicted user problems, and some potential solutions to those problems.
Problems
Solutions
- Existing hotel chain sites seem to be geared either towards frequent business travelers, or newer vacationers, and not both.

- User groups that were identified earlier, inexperienced users and experienced users, may have conflicting user goals: inexperienced users may want a very detailed process so that they feel comfortable knowing they chose the right hotel, while experienced users want a streamlined booking process.

- Due to the large amount of information needed for users to book hotels, pages can become overwhelming and cluttered, especially on mobile versions of a site.
- Have two main paths for users to follow: one generic path that gives every page of information along the way, and one for people with a free account, which lets users save their card information on file, and rebook past stays with a faster process.

- Have general hotel information for the curious available in a separate page, to free up space in the main flow.

- Spread information across several pages in chunks by topic, rather than as much as possible in a given page.

Making this site was a great opportunity for me to practice finding design solutions for groups of users with conflicting goals, and I enjoyed the extra challenge that this project provided.

If I were to do this project again, I would try to find more willing participants to test my wireframes and prototypes against, to see if any other usability issues consistently popped up. However, given that this was an independent project, I am happy with the work I've done on this project and its end result.
